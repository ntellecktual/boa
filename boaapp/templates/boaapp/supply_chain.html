Rationale

Design Decisions & Tradeoffs

Every inventory parameter encodes a business decision — here's the "why" behind the math.

Why EOQ Instead of Just-In-Time (JIT)?

JIT minimizes inventory holding costs by ordering exactly what's needed, exactly when needed. But JIT requires near-perfect demand forecasting and ultra-reliable suppliers — a single late shipment causes stockouts. Amazon's fulfillment centers handle 10,000+ SKUs with variable demand and 100+ suppliers at different reliability levels. EOQ provides a safety buffer that absorbs demand variability and supplier delays. The tradeoff: EOQ holds more inventory (higher carrying costs) but achieves 99.5% fill rates vs. JIT's typical 94% in high-variability environments.

INVENTORY STRATEGY
Why Service-Level-Based Safety Stock?

Safety stock could be a fixed quantity (always keep 100 extra units) or dynamic (computed from demand variability). Fixed stock wastes capital on low-variability items and risks stockouts on high-variability items. Service-level-based safety stock uses the Z-score for the target fill rate (Z=1.65 for 95%, Z=2.33 for 99%) multiplied by demand standard deviation × √lead time. This automatically adjusts buffer size per product: a predictable commodity gets 50 units of safety stock while a trending seasonal item gets 500.

CAPITAL OPTIMIZATION
Why Box-Muller for Demand Simulation?

Historical replay uses actual past demand data, which is more "realistic" but fails for new products (no history) and doesn't capture tail events (the 1-in-100 demand spike). Box-Muller generates normally distributed demand around the mean, with configurable standard deviation. This lets us stress-test: "What if demand variance doubles during Prime Day?" Historical replay can't answer hypotheticals. The tradeoff: Box-Muller assumes normal distribution; real demand often has fat tails (better modeled by Poisson or Negative Binomial for low-volume SKUs).

SIMULATION METHODOLOGY
Why ABC Classification for SKU Prioritization?

Not all SKUs deserve the same safety stock service level. ABC classification segments SKUs by revenue contribution: A-items (top 20% of SKUs = 80% of revenue) get 99% service level, B-items (next 30% = 15% revenue) get 95%, C-items (bottom 50% = 5% revenue) get 90%. This allocates safety stock capital where it matters most. Without ABC classification, you'd either over-invest in C-items or under-serve A-items.

RESOURCE ALLOCATION
Why Reorder Point = (Avg Demand × Lead Time) + Safety Stock?

The reorder point triggers a replenishment order when inventory drops to this level. Setting it too high wastes warehouse space and ties up capital. Setting it too low risks stockouts during the lead time window. The formula (d̄ × LT + SS) ensures that even if demand exceeds the average during the lead time period, the safety stock buffer prevents a stockout with the probability defined by the service level. The key insight: the reorder point is NOT about "when to order" — it's about "how much risk are you willing to accept during the vulnerability window between order and delivery."

EOQ + Safety Stock Calculator (Python) 
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
from dataclasses import dataclass

# ── EOQ + Safety Stock for Amazon FC replenishment ──

@dataclass
class SKUParams:
    sku_id: str
    annual_demand: float           # D: forecasted annual units
    demand_std_weekly: float      # σ: weekly demand std deviation
    lead_time_weeks: float       # L: vendor → FC transit time
    unit_cost: float              # C: landed cost per unit
    storage_cost_cuft_mo: float  # FC storage rate ($/cu ft/month)
    cube_per_unit: float         # cubic feet per unit
    supplier_moq: int            # minimum order quantity

def compute_holding_cost(p: SKUParams, capital_rate: float = 0.12,
                         obsolescence_rate: float = 0.03) -> float:
    """Annual holding cost per unit = capital + storage + obsolescence."""
    capital = p.unit_cost * capital_rate
    storage = p.storage_cost_cuft_mo * p.cube_per_unit * 12
    obsolescence = p.unit_cost * obsolescence_rate
    return capital + storage + obsolescence

def compute_ordering_cost(po_processing: float = 45.0,
                          freight_per_order: float = 280.0,
                          receiving_labor: float = 25.0) -> float:
    """Fixed cost per purchase order placed."""
    return po_processing + freight_per_order + receiving_labor

def eoq_with_moq(D: float, S: float, H: float, moq: int) -> int:
    """EOQ rounded up to supplier minimum order quantity."""
    raw_eoq = np.sqrt(2 * D * S / H)
    return max(moq, int(np.ceil(raw_eoq / moq) * moq))

def safety_stock(sigma_weekly: float, lead_time: float,
                  service_level: float = 0.95) -> int:
    """Z-score safety stock: SS = Z × σ × √LT"""
    z = stats.norm.ppf(service_level)  # 0.95 → 1.645, 0.99 → 2.326
    return int(np.ceil(z * sigma_weekly * np.sqrt(lead_time)))

def reorder_point(avg_weekly_demand: float, lead_time: float,
                   ss: int) -> int:
    """ROP = d̄ × LT + Safety Stock"""
    return int(np.ceil(avg_weekly_demand * lead_time + ss))

def total_annual_cost(D: float, Q: int, S: float,
                       H: float, ss: int) -> float:
    """TC = ordering cost + holding cost (cycle stock + safety stock)"""
    ordering = (D / Q) * S
    holding = (Q / 2 + ss) * H
    return ordering + holding

# ── Example: Echo Dot 5th Gen at BOS5 ──
params = SKUParams(
    sku_id="B09B8V1LZ3", annual_demand=52000,
    demand_std_weekly=85, lead_time_weeks=2,
    unit_cost=22.99, storage_cost_cuft_mo=0.87,
    cube_per_unit=0.18, supplier_moq=500
)
H = compute_holding_cost(params)        # ~$5.65/unit/yr
S = compute_ordering_cost()              # $350/order
Q = eoq_with_moq(params.annual_demand, S, H, params.supplier_moq)
ss = safety_stock(params.demand_std_weekly, params.lead_time_weeks)
rop = reorder_point(params.annual_demand/52, params.lead_time_weeks, ss)
tc = total_annual_cost(params.annual_demand, Q, S, H, ss)
print(f"EOQ={Q}, SS={ss}, ROP={rop}, TC=${tc:,.0f}")
Demand Forecasting — Holt-Winters Triple Exponential Smoothing (Python) 
import numpy as np
from scipy.optimize import minimize

class HoltWinters:
    """Triple exponential smoothing: level + trend + seasonality."""

    def __init__(self, season_len: int = 52):
        self.m = season_len  # 52 weeks for weekly data

    def fit(self, y: np.ndarray, alpha=0.3, beta=0.05, gamma=0.15):
        """Fit model to historical demand series y."""
        m, n = self.m, len(y)
        # Initialize level, trend, seasonal
        level = np.mean(y[:m])
        trend = (np.mean(y[m:2*m]) - np.mean(y[:m])) / m
        seasonal = np.array([y[i] - level for i in range(m)])

        self.levels, self.trends, self.seasons = [level], [trend], list(seasonal)
        self.fitted = []

        for t in range(n):
            if t >= m:
                forecast = (level + trend) * seasonal[t % m]  # multiplicative
            else:
                forecast = y[t]
            self.fitted.append(forecast)

            prev_level = level
            level = alpha * (y[t] / seasonal[t % m]) + (1 - alpha) * (level + trend)
            trend = beta * (level - prev_level) + (1 - beta) * trend
            seasonal[t % m] = gamma * (y[t] / level) + (1 - gamma) * seasonal[t % m]

            self.levels.append(level)
            self.trends.append(trend)

        self.level, self.trend = level, trend
        self.seasonal = seasonal
        return self

    def forecast(self, horizon: int) -> np.ndarray:
        """Predict next `horizon` periods."""
        preds = []
        for h in range(1, horizon + 1):
            yhat = (self.level + h * self.trend) * self.seasonal[(len(self.fitted) + h) % self.m]
            preds.append(max(0, yhat))
        return np.array(preds)

    def error_metrics(self, y: np.ndarray) -> dict:
        """MAE and MAPE on fitted values."""
        fitted = np.array(self.fitted[self.m:])
        actual = y[self.m:]
        mae = np.mean(np.abs(actual - fitted))
        mape = np.mean(np.abs((actual - fitted) / actual)) * 100
        return {"MAE": round(mae, 2), "MAPE": round(mape, 2)}

def optimize_params(y: np.ndarray, season_len: int = 52) -> dict:
    """Grid search for optimal alpha, beta, gamma minimizing MAE."""
    best_mae, best_params = float('inf'), {}
    for a in np.arange(0.05, 0.5, 0.05):
        for b in np.arange(0.01, 0.2, 0.02):
            for g in np.arange(0.05, 0.4, 0.05):
                hw = HoltWinters(season_len).fit(y, a, b, g)
                mae = hw.error_metrics(y)["MAE"]
                if mae < best_mae:
                    best_mae = mae
                    best_params = {"alpha": a, "beta": b, "gamma": g}
    return best_params

Multi-Echelon Inventory Optimizer — Clark-Scarf Decomposition (Python) 
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
from dataclasses import dataclass
from typing import List

# ── Multi-Echelon: Central Warehouse → Regional DC → FC ──

@dataclass
class Echelon:
    name: str
    lead_time: float           # replenishment lead time in weeks
    demand_mean_weekly: float  # mean downstream demand per week
    demand_std_weekly: float   # std dev of weekly demand
    holding_cost: float        # $/unit/week at this echelon
    service_level: float       # target fill rate

def echelon_safety_stock(e: Echelon) -> int:
    """Safety stock at each echelon covers its own lead time variability."""
    z = stats.norm.ppf(e.service_level)
    # Demand variability over echelon-specific lead time
    sigma_lt = e.demand_std_weekly * np.sqrt(e.lead_time)
    return int(np.ceil(z * sigma_lt))

def echelon_base_stock(e: Echelon) -> int:
    """Base-stock level = expected demand during LT + safety stock."""
    pipeline = e.demand_mean_weekly * e.lead_time
    ss = echelon_safety_stock(e)
    return int(np.ceil(pipeline + ss))

def optimize_network(echelons: List[Echelon]) -> dict:
    """Clark-Scarf decomposition: optimize each echelon independently.
    Valid when costs are nested and demand propagates downstream."""
    results = {}
    total_ss_cost = 0
    for e in echelons:
        ss = echelon_safety_stock(e)
        base = echelon_base_stock(e)
        weekly_cost = ss * e.holding_cost
        results[e.name] = {
            "safety_stock": ss,
            "base_stock_level": base,
            "weekly_holding_cost": round(weekly_cost, 2),
            "service_level": f"{e.service_level*100:.1f}%"
        }
        total_ss_cost += weekly_cost
    results["total_weekly_ss_cost"] = round(total_ss_cost, 2)
    return results

# ── Amazon network: 3-echelon for Echo Dot ──
network = [
    Echelon("Central Warehouse (ONT8)",
            lead_time=4, demand_mean_weekly=8500,
            demand_std_weekly=600, holding_cost=0.08,
            service_level=0.99),
    Echelon("Regional DC (BOS1)",
            lead_time=1.5, demand_mean_weekly=2100,
            demand_std_weekly=180, holding_cost=0.12,
            service_level=0.97),
    Echelon("Fulfillment Center (BOS5)",
            lead_time=2, demand_mean_weekly=1000,
            demand_std_weekly=85, holding_cost=0.16,
            service_level=0.95),
]

result = optimize_network(network)
for name, data in result.items():
    if name != "total_weekly_ss_cost":
        print(f"{name}: SS={data['safety_stock']}, Base={data['base_stock_level']}")
print(f"Total weekly SS holding cost: ${result['total_weekly_ss_cost']:,.2f}")

Innovation Spotlight

Anticipatory Shipping

Amazon's patented approach: using predictive models to ship products to regional warehouses BEFORE customers order them. The model estimates P(order | customer, product, time) and ships when the expected value of pre-positioning (faster delivery × conversion lift) exceeds the expected shipping cost if the prediction is wrong. A single day of delivery speed improvement increases conversion by ~1.5% for Prime-eligible items — making speculative shipping profitable even at 60% prediction accuracy for high-velocity SKUs.

Dynamic Safety Stock with Demand Sensing — Instead of static safety stock based on historical variance, the system ingests real-time signals: weather forecasts (storms → generator demand), social media trends (viral TikTok → product spikes), and promotional calendars. Safety stock automatically increases 48 hours before predicted demand surges, preventing the stockout-during-promotion failure mode. At Amazon's scale, demand sensing reduces excess inventory by 12% while improving fill rates by 1.8 percentage points — the rare optimization that improves both cost and service simultaneously.
